### 03-decision-making-frameworks.md

**Purpose**: Provide mental models for making tough prioritization decisions between competing options.

**Content:**

1. **Opportunity Cost Analysis**
   - Definition: Value of next best alternative foregone
   - Every choice has an opportunity cost
   - Making opportunity costs explicit
   - Opportunity cost questions:
     - "If I say yes to this, what am I saying no to?"
     - "What could I do with this time/money/energy instead?"
   - Example analyses (should I take this meeting? build this feature? pursue this side project?)

2. **Trade-off Assessment**
   - Trade-offs are inevitable in prioritization
   - You can't have everything; you must choose
   - Trade-off mapping exercise
   - Pros/cons lists (improved methodology)
   - Multi-criteria decision matrix (scoring multiple options across factors)

3. **First Principles Thinking (Elon Musk)**
   - What it is: Reasoning from fundamental truths, not analogies
   - Musk's process: "Boil things down to most fundamental truths, then reason up"
   - Breaking down assumptions
   - Example: Battery costs (everyone said expensive forever; Musk questioned fundamental materials cost)
   - Applying to prioritization:
     - "Why are we doing this at all?"
     - "What problem are we actually solving?"
     - "What would we do if we started from scratch?"
   - When to use: Challenging conventional wisdom, making big strategic choices

4. **Regret Minimization Framework (Jeff Bezos)**
   - The origin story (leaving Wall Street to start Amazon)
   - The core question: "When I'm 80, will I regret not doing this?"
   - Projection to future self
   - Separates from present-day fears
   - Applying to career decisions, life choices, risk-taking
   - Short-term pain vs. long-term regret trade-off
   - When to use: Major life decisions with high uncertainty

5. **Second-Order Thinking**
   - First-order: Immediate consequences
   - Second-order: Consequences of the consequences
   - Third-order: And so on...
   - Example: Say yes to all requests → short-term popularity but long-term mediocrity
   - Questions to ask:
     - "And then what?"
     - "What happens after that?"
     - "What are the unintended consequences?"

6. **Reversibility Analysis (Type 1 vs. Type 2 Decisions)**
   - Jeff Bezos's framework
   - Type 1: Irreversible or very difficult to reverse (one-way doors) → Slow, careful
   - Type 2: Reversible (two-way doors) → Fast, experimental
   - Most decisions are Type 2, but we treat them like Type 1
   - Prioritization implication: Move fast on reversible decisions

7. **When to Use Which Framework**
   - Decision tree / framework selection guide
   - Opportunity cost: Resource allocation decisions
   - First principles: Challenging status quo, strategic pivots
   - Regret minimization: Career/life changes, big bets
   - Reversibility: Balancing speed vs. thoroughness

**Timeline**: Apply immediately to specific decisions

**Templates Referenced**: `decision-matrix-template`, `opportunity-cost-worksheet`, `regret-minimization-script`

### 04-product-feature-prioritization.md ⭐ COMPREHENSIVE

**Purpose**: Master data-driven frameworks for prioritizing product features, roadmap items, and backlog.

**Content:**

1. **Why Product Prioritization Matters**
   - Limited resources (time, engineering, budget)
   - Unlimited ideas and requests
   - Impact of wrong priorities (opportunity cost, technical debt, team morale)
   - Data-driven > opinion-driven prioritization

2. **RICE Framework (Intercom)**
   - **R**each: How many users/customers affected (per quarter/year)
   - **I**mpact: How much it affects each user (3=massive, 2=high, 1=medium, 0.5=low, 0.25=minimal)
   - **C**onfidence: How certain are you? (100%=high, 80%=medium, 50%=low)
   - **E**ffort: Person-months of work
   - **Formula**: (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
   - Step-by-step process
   - Example calculations
   - When to use: Ready-made product with data on reach/impact
   - Limitations: Requires good data, confidence can be subjective

3. **ICE Framework**
   - Simplified RICE (no Reach dimension)
   - **I**mpact (1-10)
   - **C**onfidence (1-10)
   - **E**ase (inverse of effort, 1-10)
   - **Formula**: (Impact × Confidence × Ease) / 3
   - When to use: Quick MVP prioritization, early stage, limited data
   - Faster than RICE but less rigorous

4. **MoSCoW Method**
   - **M**ust have: Non-negotiable, product fails without it
   - **S**hould have: Important but not critical
   - **C**ould have: Nice to have, include if time permits
   - **W**on't have: Explicitly out of scope (for now)
   - Forcing function: Limit Must-haves to 60% of capacity
   - When to use: Stakeholder alignment, release planning, setting expectations
   - Best for: Small teams, projects with clear requirements
   - Limitation: Less granular than scoring methods

5. **WSJF (Weighted Shortest Job First)**
   - From SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)
   - **Cost of Delay (CoD)** = User/Business Value + Time Criticality + Risk Reduction/Opportunity Enablement
   - User/Business Value: Revenue potential, user impact (1-10)
   - Time Criticality: How fast value decays (1-10)
   - Risk Reduction: Mitigating risk or enabling future work (1-10)
   - **Job Size**: Effort estimate (story points, person-days)
   - **Formula**: CoD / Job Size
   - When to use: Cost of delay is critical, agile teams, large backlogs
   - Prioritizes "quick wins" and time-sensitive items

6. **CD3 (Cost of Delay Divided by Duration)**
   - Simplified WSJF
   - CD3 = Revenue potential / (Months to implement × 1000)
   - Maximize total value in given time period
   - Highest CD3 = fastest ROI
   - When to use: Economic prioritization, portfolio management

7. **Kano Model**
   - Developed by Noriaki Kano (1980s)
   - Three feature categories:
     - **Basic/Must-Have**: Expected, no delight if present, huge dissatisfaction if absent (e.g., turn signal in car)
     - **Performance**: Satisfaction scales linearly with investment (e.g., speed, ease of use)
     - **Excitement/Delighters**: Not expected; disproportionate delight if present, no dissatisfaction if absent (e.g., surprise features)
   - Two additional categories: Indifferent, Reverse
   - Features migrate over time (excitement → performance → basic)
   - Example: AI features (were excitement, becoming performance/basic in 2025)
   - When to use: Customer satisfaction focus, feature mix strategy
   - Best for: Understanding customer expectations
   - Process: Customer surveys with functional/dysfunctional question pairs

8. **Value vs. Effort Matrix (2×2)**
   - X-axis: Effort (Low → High)
   - Y-axis: Value (Low → High)
   - Four quadrants:
     - **High Value / Low Effort**: Quick wins (do first)
     - **High Value / High Effort**: Big bets (strategic investment)
     - **Low Value / Low Effort**: Fill-ins (do if time)
     - **Low Value / High Effort**: Money pits (avoid)
   - Visual, intuitive, fast
   - When to use: Stakeholder workshops, simple visual needed
   - Limitation: Less precise than scoring

9. **Buy a Feature (Innovation Games)**
   - Collaborative prioritization game by Luke Hohmann
   - Give customers/stakeholders "money" (monopoly money, chips)
   - Each feature has a price tag (based on development cost)
   - Customers "buy" features they value most
   - Money is limited (usually 1/3 to 2/3 of total feature cost) → forces prioritization
   - Customers must pool money for expensive features → reveals coalitions
   - When to use: Customer input needed, stakeholder alignment, team consensus
   - Can be done in-person or virtually (Miro, Mural)

10. **Framework Comparison Matrix**
    - Table comparing all frameworks:
      - Best for: (type of product, stage, team)
      - Requires: (data needs)
      - Speed: (fast, medium, slow)
      - Quantitative vs. Qualitative
      - Stakeholder involvement
      - Pros/Cons

11. **Implementation Guide**
    - How to introduce prioritization framework to organization
    - Getting buy-in from leadership
    - Training the team
    - Pilot program approach
    - Common objections and responses

12. **Case Studies**
    - Real examples with specific results
    - Before/after scenarios
    - Lessons learned

**Timeline**: 2-4 weeks to implement framework organization-wide

**Templates Referenced**: `rice-scoring-spreadsheet`, `ice-calculator`, `moscow-matrix`, `wsjf-calculator`, `value-effort-matrix`, `buy-a-feature-board`

### 05-stakeholder-management-prioritization.md ⭐ COMPREHENSIVE

**Purpose**: Navigate conflicting stakeholder demands and build consensus on priorities.

**Content:**

1. **The Stakeholder Prioritization Challenge**
   - Multiple stakeholders, multiple priorities
   - Everyone thinks their request is most important
   - Saying no feels risky politically
   - Lack of objective criteria leads to loudest voice winning
   - HiPPO problem (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)

2. **Early Alignment on Shared Goals**
   - Amazon's "working backward" method
   - Start with customer outcome, work backward to requirements
   - Define success criteria upfront
   - Get agreement on what matters before discussing how

3. **Prioritization Scoring Models**
   - Objective criteria reduce politics
   - Common criteria: Value, Urgency, Feasibility, Strategic Alignment
   - Weight criteria based on company priorities
   - Score each request 1-10 on each criterion
   - Calculate weighted score
   - Highest scores = highest priority
   - Make scoring transparent to all stakeholders

4. **Data-Driven Prioritization Frameworks**
   - Use RICE, WSJF, or similar for stakeholder requests
   - Customer data trumps opinions
   - Usage data, revenue data, customer feedback
   - "We prioritize based on impact to customers, measured by..."

5. **Using Customer Voice to Align Stakeholders**
   - Customer quotes, recordings, data
   - Hard for stakeholders to argue with customer voice
   - Customer advisory boards
   - Quarterly customer prioritization exercises
   - Win/loss analysis (why do we lose deals?)

6. **Communicating Trade-Offs Effectively**
   - Never just say "no" - explain the trade-off
   - "If we do X, we can't do Y" (make opportunity cost explicit)
   - Use roadmap visualization
   - Show capacity constraints visually
   - Scenario planning: "Here's what we can do with current resources vs. 2x resources"

7. **Building Consensus Without Compromise**
   - Consensus ≠ everyone gets what they want
   - Consensus = everyone understands why decision was made
   - Disagree and commit (Amazon principle)
   - Techniques:
     - Weighted voting
     - Forced ranking exercises
     - Buy a Feature game with stakeholders
     - Affinity mapping (group similar requests)

8. **Handling HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)**
   - Problem: Executive opinion overrides data/process
   - Solutions:
     - Pre-align with executive one-on-one
     - Get executive to agree to prioritization framework upfront
     - Show data that contradicts opinion (respectfully)
     - "Help me understand why you think X is higher priority than data suggests?"
     - Frame as learning ("Let's test your hypothesis")

9. **Stakeholder Influence Mapping**
   - Power/Interest matrix
   - High Power/High Interest: Manage closely
   - High Power/Low Interest: Keep satisfied
   - Low Power/High Interest: Keep informed
   - Low Power/Low Interest: Monitor
   - Tailor communication by quadrant

10. **Regular Prioritization Rituals**
    - Quarterly roadmap planning with key stakeholders
    - Monthly backlog grooming
    - Weekly stakeholder updates
    - Consistent process builds trust

11. **Saying No to Powerful Stakeholders**
    - Link to: 07-saying-no-professionally.md
    - Specific scripts for executive requests
    - "Yes, and" vs. "Yes, but" vs. "No, because"

12. **Case Studies**
    - Navigating VP-level conflicting priorities
    - Using customer data to deprioritize CEO pet project
    - Building consensus in cross-functional team

**Timeline**: Apply immediately in stakeholder conflicts

**Templates Referenced**: `stakeholder-prioritization-map`, `scoring-model-template`, `roadmap-visualization-template`

### 06-delegation-mastery.md

**Purpose**: Learn to delegate effectively as a form of prioritization—doing high-value work through others.

**Content:**

1. **Delegation as Prioritization**
   - You can't do everything yourself
   - Delegation = focusing your time on highest-value work
   - The cost of not delegating: Burnout, bottlenecks, underdeveloped team

2. **When to Delegate vs. Do Yourself**
   - Delegation decision matrix:
     - High value to org + Only you can do = Do yourself
     - High value + Others can do = Delegate with oversight
     - Low value + Others can do = Delegate fully
     - Low value + Only you can do = Automate or eliminate
   - The Eisenhower Matrix for delegation:
     - Q1 (Urgent/Important): Do or delegate to senior team member
     - Q2 (Not Urgent/Important): Do yourself (strategic work)
     - Q3 (Urgent/Not Important): Delegate (other people's priorities)
     - Q4 (Not Urgent/Not Important): Eliminate

3. **What to Delegate**
   - Tasks below your pay grade
   - Tasks others can do 80% as well
   - Tasks that develop others
   - Repetitive tasks
   - Tasks that don't require your unique expertise
   - What NOT to delegate:
     - Core strategic decisions
     - Personnel issues (hiring, firing, sensitive feedback)
     - Tasks only you can do (unique expertise)
     - Vision and culture setting

4. **Matching Tasks to Team Member Strengths**
   - Know your team's capabilities and preferences
   - Delegation as development opportunity
   - Stretch assignments vs. comfort zone tasks
   - Interest alignment increases motivation

5. **Four Levels of Delegation** (MIT Sloan)
   - **Level 1: Tell** - "Do exactly this" (low autonomy)
   - **Level 2: Sell** - Explain why, but prescribe how (medium autonomy)
   - **Level 3: Consult** - Seek input, then decide together (high autonomy)
   - **Level 4: Delegate** - Full ownership, check in on results (highest autonomy)
   - Match level to task complexity and team member capability

6. **The Delegation Conversation**
   - Context: Why does this matter?
   - Outcome: What does success look like?
   - Constraints: What are the boundaries?
   - Authority: What decisions can they make?
   - Resources: What support do they have?
   - Timeline: When is it due? Checkpoints?
   - Questions: What's unclear?

7. **Follow-Up Without Micromanaging**
   - Agree on checkpoint schedule upfront
   - Ask "How's it going?" not "Did you do X?"
   - Focus on outcomes, not methods
   - Create psychological safety for questions/problems
   - Trust but verify (especially early in relationship)

8. **Common Delegation Failures**
   - Unclear expectations → Rework
   - Delegating outcome but micromanaging process → Frustration
   - No follow-up → Surprises at deadline
   - Delegating to wrong person → Poor results
   - Reverse delegation (taking it back) → Dependency
   - Rescuing too quickly → Team doesn't learn

9. **Building a Culture of Delegation**
   - Model delegation (show it's not about dumping work)
   - Celebrate successful delegated projects
   - Develop delegation skills in your team (they should delegate too)
   - Make it safe to ask for help

10. **Case Studies**
    - Manager who couldn't let go (consequences)
    - Effective delegation that developed future leader
    - Data-driven delegation strategy (30% increase in project completion)

**Timeline**: 2-3 weeks to build delegation systems

**Templates Referenced**: `delegation-decision-matrix`, `delegation-conversation-template`, `delegation-tracking-sheet`

### 07-saying-no-professionally.md

**Purpose**: Master the skill of saying no gracefully to protect focus and priorities.

**Content:**

1. **Why Saying No is Strategic Prioritization**
   - Every yes is a no to something else
   - Warren Buffett: "The difference between successful people and really successful people is that really successful people say no to almost everything"
   - Greg McKeown: "If you don't prioritize your life, someone else will"
   - The cost of saying yes to everything: Mediocrity, burnout, missed opportunities

2. **The Psychology of Saying No**
   - Why it's hard: Fear of disappointing, FOMO, desire to be liked, optimism bias (overestimate capacity)
   - Reframing: Saying no is saying yes to your priorities
   - Boundary-setting as self-respect

3. **The "No" Spectrum**
   - Hard no: "I can't do this"
   - Soft no: "Not now, but maybe later"
   - Conditional yes: "Yes, if we deprioritize X"
   - Redirect: "I can't, but [person Y] could"
   - Defer: "Let me think about it" (buy time)

4. **Scripts for Saying No Professionally**
   - **To peers:**
     - "I'd love to help, but I'm at capacity with [current priorities]. Can we revisit in [timeframe]?"
     - "I'm not the best person for this—have you considered [alternative]?"
   - **To managers:**
     - "I'm currently working on X, Y, Z. If I take this on, which should I deprioritize?"
     - "I can do this, but it will push [other project] by [timeframe]. Which is more important?"
   - **To executives:**
     - "To give this the attention it deserves, I'd need to pause [current priority]. Is that the right trade-off?"
     - "I want to make sure I understand the priority. Is this more important than [other exec initiative]?"
   - **To customers/external:**
     - "That's outside our current scope, but we can explore it for [future date]."
     - "We've prioritized X based on feedback from customers like you. Here's why..."

5. **The "I" Statement Framework**
   - Use "I" statements to own your boundaries
   - "I'm currently focused on..."
   - "I need to prioritize..."
   - "I won't be able to..."
   - Avoids blame, takes ownership

6. **Offering Alternatives Without Taking On Work**
   - Suggest someone else
   - Provide resources (articles, templates)
   - Offer limited help ("I can spend 15 mins advising, but can't execute")
   - Timebox ("I can help after [date]")

7. **Saying No to Meetings**
   - "Can we handle this async?"
   - "What's the goal of this meeting? Is my attendance necessary?"
   - Accept for first 15 minutes only
   - Delegate a team member

8. **Saying No to Scope Creep**
   - "That's a great idea for v2, but out of scope for v1"
   - Document what's in/out of scope upfront
   - Change request process

9. **The Power of "Not Now"**
   - Softer than no, but still protective
   - "I'm prioritizing X this quarter. Let's revisit next quarter."
   - Keep a "later" list to capture ideas

10. **When You Can't Say No**
    - Negotiate timeline
    - Negotiate scope
    - Negotiate resources (get help)
    - Surface the trade-off explicitly
    - Document the decision (CYA)

11. **Building Your "No" Muscle**
    - Start small (practice with low-stakes requests)
    - Role play with trusted colleague
    - Reflect on past yeses you regret
    - Weekly: Count your nos (track progress)

12. **Cultural Considerations**
    - Saying no in different company cultures
    - Saying no across hierarchies
    - International considerations

**Timeline**: Practice daily for 3-4 weeks to build confidence

**Templates Referenced**: `saying-no-scripts`, `boundary-setting-guide`

### 08-time-blocking-deep-work.md ⭐ COMPREHENSIVE

**Purpose**: Implement Cal Newport's time blocking method to protect focus and accomplish deep work.

**Content:**

1. **The Problem: Reactive vs. Proactive Time**
   - Most people live reactively (email, Slack, meetings)
   - Reactive mode = other people's priorities
   - Proactive mode = your priorities
   - Research: Time blockers accomplish ~2× more per week

2. **What is Time Blocking?**
   - Cal Newport's method: Assign every minute a job
   - Plan your day in 30-minute blocks
   - Include everything: Deep work, meetings, lunch, email, breaks
   - Physical or digital planner

3. **Deep Work vs. Shallow Work**
   - **Deep work**: Cognitively demanding, creates value, hard to replicate (writing, coding, strategic thinking, problem-solving)
   - **Shallow work**: Not cognitively demanding, easy to replicate (email, meetings, admin, some Slack)
   - Deep work drives career success; shallow work is necessary but not sufficient

4. **The Time Blocking Process**
   - **Step 1**: List all tasks (deep and shallow)
   - **Step 2**: Estimate time for each
   - **Step 3**: Assign to time blocks
   - **Step 4**: Protect deep work blocks (no meetings, no email)
   - **Step 5**: Be realistic about shallow work time needed
   - **Step 6**: Leave buffer time (things take longer than expected)
   - **Step 7**: Review at end of day, adjust for tomorrow

5. **Scheduling Deep Work**
   - Schedule deep work during your peak energy hours (see: 09-energy-management-chronotypes.md)
   - Minimum 90-minute blocks (cognitive switching costs are high)
   - Ideal: 2-4 hour blocks
   - Protect with calendar holds
   - Communicate boundaries (e.g., "Deep work, available after 2pm")

6. **Batching Shallow Work**
   - Group similar tasks (email, Slack, admin, meetings)
   - Email batching: 3× per day (morning, midday, end of day)
   - Meeting batching: Certain days/times only
   - Reduces context switching

7. **Handling Interruptions**
   - Anticipate and schedule interrupt time
   - "Office hours" for questions
   - Out-of-office message with availability
   - Protect deep work blocks (close Slack, email, phone)

8. **The Art of Flexible Time Blocking**
   - Plans change—that's okay
   - When interrupted, re-block the rest of the day
   - Don't abandon system because one block failed
   - Learn from mismatches (task took 2hrs, you blocked 1hr)

9. **Time Blocking Templates**
   - Maker schedule (long deep work blocks)
   - Manager schedule (meetings + some deep work)
   - Hybrid schedule (manager time some days, maker time other days)
   - Weekly recurring blocks (planning, review, deep work)

10. **Tools for Time Blocking**
    - Paper planner (Cal Newport's Time Block Planner)
    - Digital calendar (Google Cal, Outlook)
    - Dedicated apps (Reclaim, Motion, Plan)
    - Timeboxing techniques

11. **Common Pitfalls**
    - Over-optimistic time estimates
    - No buffer time
    - Scheduling too much deep work (4hrs/day max for most people)
    - Not protecting blocks (letting meetings encroach)
    - Abandoning system after one bad day

12. **Case Studies**
    - Cal Newport's personal time blocking practice
    - Developer who doubled productivity with deep work blocks
    - Executive who reclaimed 10hrs/week

**Timeline**: 2-3 weeks to establish time blocking habit

### 09-energy-management-chronotypes.md

**Purpose**: Optimize productivity by aligning tasks with natural energy rhythms and chronotype.

**Content:**

1. **Energy > Time**
   - Time is fixed; energy fluctuates
   - All hours are not equal
   - Peak energy hours are precious—protect them
   - Working against your chronotype reduces effectiveness by 50%+

2. **Understanding Chronotypes**
   - Biological prime time (BPT) = when you think best
   - Four chronotypes:
     - **Lions** (15-20% of population): Wake 5-6am, peak 8am-12pm, crash by 8pm
     - **Bears** (50%): Wake 7-8am, peak 10am-2pm, dip 2-4pm, taper 9pm (aligned with sun)
     - **Wolves** (15-20%): Wake 9-10am, sluggish AM, peak 5pm-midnight
     - **Dolphins** (10%): Light sleepers, peak mid-morning to early afternoon
   - Based on genetics, age, gender

3. **Identifying Your Chronotype**
   - Online assessments (Power of When quiz)
   - Track energy for one week (1-10 rating every hour)
   - When do you naturally wake without alarm (on vacation)?
   - When do you feel most alert?
   - When do you prefer to exercise, socialize, do hard thinking?

4. **Aligning Tasks with Energy Levels**
   - **Peak energy**: Deep work, strategic thinking, creative work, difficult conversations, important decisions
   - **Medium energy**: Meetings, collaboration, email, planning
   - **Low energy**: Admin, routine tasks, inbox zero, breaks
   - Example for Lion:
     - 6-8am: Deep work (writing, strategy)
     - 8-10am: Meetings (still high energy)
     - 10-12pm: Collaborative work
     - 12-1pm: Lunch, walk
     - 1-3pm: Admin, email (post-lunch dip)
     - 3-5pm: Finish up, plan tomorrow
     - 5pm: Done for the day

5. **The Post-Lunch Dip**
   - Universal: Most people dip 1-3pm
   - Circadian rhythm, not just food
   - Strategies:
     - Schedule low-cognitive tasks
     - Take a walk
     - Power nap (20 mins)
     - Don't schedule important meetings

6. **Energy Renewal Practices**
   - Physical: Exercise, sleep, nutrition, hydration
   - Mental: Breaks, meditation, nature, hobbies
   - Emotional: Social connection, laughter, gratitude
   - Spiritual: Purpose, meaning, values alignment
   - Recovery is not wasted time—it's performance fuel

7. **Sleep Optimization**
   - Most important factor for energy
   - 7-9 hours for most adults
   - Consistent sleep/wake times
   - Sleep hygiene: Dark, cool, quiet, no screens 1hr before

8. **Strategic Caffeine Use**
   - Wait 90-120 mins after waking (let cortisol clear)
   - Use during mid-morning dip (10am for most)
   - Avoid after 2pm (disrupts sleep)
   - Caffeine masks fatigue, doesn't create energy

9. **Weekly Energy Planning**
   - Some days are "maker days" (deep work focus)
   - Some days are "manager days" (meetings, collaboration)
   - Protect at least 2 maker days per week
   - Schedule demanding tasks on high-energy days

10. **When Your Schedule Doesn't Match Your Chronotype**

    - Strategies:
      - Negotiate flexible hours if possible
      - Protect peak hours ruthlessly for deep work
      - Use light therapy in morning (shifts circadian rhythm slightly)
      - Accept some mismatch (advocate for change when you can)

11. **Tracking and Optimizing**
    - Energy audit: Track for 2 weeks
    - Identify patterns
    - Experiment with task timing
    - Measure productivity during different times

### 10-eisenhower-matrix-daily-planning.md

**Purpose**: Master the urgent/important distinction for daily task prioritization.

**Content:**

1. **The Eisenhower Insight**
   - "What is important is seldom urgent, and what is urgent is seldom important" - Dwight D. Eisenhower
   - Most people live in urgent mode
   - True effectiveness comes from important mode

2. **The Four Quadrants**

   ```
            │ Urgent │ Not Urgent
   ─────────┼────────┼──────────
   Important│   Q1   │    Q2
            │ Crisis │  Planning
   ─────────┼────────┼──────────
   Not      │   Q3   │    Q4
   Important│  Noise │   Waste
   ```

   - **Q1: Urgent & Important** (Crisis/Emergency)
     - Examples: Deadline tomorrow, server down, emergency meeting, customer escalation
     - How to handle: Do immediately
     - Goal: Minimize time here

   - **Q2: Not Urgent & Important** (Strategic/Proactive)
     - Examples: Planning, relationship building, learning, exercise, deep work, prevention
     - How to handle: Schedule time, protect it
     - Goal: Maximize time here (50%+ of your time)
     - **This is where effectiveness lives**

   - **Q3: Urgent & Not Important** (Distraction/Interruption)
     - Examples: Some emails, some meetings, some calls, other people's priorities
     - How to handle: Delegate, minimize, batch
     - Goal: Eliminate or delegate most of these
     - **Common trap: Feels productive but isn't**

   - **Q4: Not Urgent & Not Important** (Waste)
     - Examples: Busywork, excessive social media, pointless meetings, trivia
     - How to handle: Eliminate completely
     - Goal: Zero time here

3. **The Q2 Focus Strategy**
   - Spending more time in Q2 prevents Q1 crises
   - Planning prevents emergencies
   - Relationship building prevents conflict
   - Learning prevents obsolescence
   - Exercise prevents illness
   - How to create Q2 time:
     - Schedule it (time blocking)
     - Protect it (say no to Q3/Q4)
     - Start with 1-2hrs per day

4. **Categorizing Tasks Correctly**
   - Common mistakes:
     - Everything feels urgent (false urgency)
     - Confusing Q3 (urgent/not important) with Q1 (urgent/important)
     - Ignoring Q2 because nothing is on fire
   - Questions to ask:
     - "What happens if I don't do this today?" (tests urgency)
     - "Does this move me toward my goals?" (tests importance)
     - "Whose priority is this?" (yours vs. someone else's)

5. **Daily Planning with Eisenhower Matrix**
   - Morning ritual (5-10 mins):
     - List all tasks
     - Categorize into Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4
     - Eliminate Q4
     - Delegate Q3 (or batch into 30-min block)
     - Schedule Q2 (protect time)
     - Handle Q1 as needed
   - Prioritize Q2 and Q1, minimize Q3, eliminate Q4

6. **Integration with Other Systems**
   - Eisenhower + Time Blocking: Block Q2 time first
   - Eisenhower + GTD: Use during weekly review to prioritize projects
   - Eisenhower + Delegation: Q3 tasks are prime delegation candidates

7. **Weekly Eisenhower Review**
   - At end of week: Where did you actually spend time?
   - Too much Q1? → Need more Q2 (prevention)
   - Too much Q3? → Need better boundaries, delegation
   - Any Q4? → Eliminate immediately
   - Not enough Q2? → Schedule it explicitly next week

8. **The Q1/Q2 Reduction Flywheel**
   - More Q2 time → Less Q1 crises
   - Less Q1 crises → More time for Q2
   - Virtuous cycle

9. **Common Objections**
   - "But my job is all Q1!" → Probably not; audit your time
   - "I can't ignore urgent requests!" → Some are Q3, not Q1
   - "Q2 feels less productive" → It's the most productive, just less visible

10. **Case Studies**
    - Executive who moved from 70% Q1/Q3 to 60% Q2 (results)
    - Team that reduced crises by 50% through Q2 planning

**Timeline**: 1-2 weeks to master categorization

**Templates Referenced**: `eisenhower-matrix-template`, `daily-planning-worksheet`

### 11-gtd-getting-things-done.md ⭐ COMPREHENSIVE

**Purpose**: Implement David Allen's comprehensive personal productivity system.

**Content:**

1. **GTD Overview**
   - Created by David Allen (2001, updated 2015)
   - Goal: "Mind like water" - stress-free productivity
   - Core insight: Your mind is for having ideas, not holding them
   - Trusted system captures everything so you can focus

2. **The Five Steps of GTD**

   **STEP 1: CAPTURE**
   - Collect everything with your attention
   - Inbox for physical items
   - Digital inbox for emails, notes, ideas
   - Voice memos, text messages to yourself
   - Capture tools: Notebook, app, email to self
   - Rule: Capture it ALL (don't filter yet)
   - Goal: Empty your head

   **STEP 2: CLARIFY**
   - Process each item: What is it? Is it actionable?
   - If NOT actionable:
     - Trash (delete)
     - Someday/Maybe (defer)
     - Reference (file for later)
   - If actionable:
     - Next action: What's the very next physical action?
     - Less than 2 mins? Do it now (2-minute rule)
     - Can someone else do it? Delegate (Waiting For list)
     - More than one step? Make it a project
     - Requires you? Add to Next Actions list with context

   **STEP 3: ORGANIZE**
   - Lists:
     - Next Actions (by context: @computer, @phone, @errands, @office)
     - Projects (anything requiring >1 action)
     - Waiting For (delegated or dependent on others)
     - Someday/Maybe (ideas for later)
     - Calendar (time-specific items only)
     - Reference (non-actionable info)
   - Key: Choose list by context and energy

   **STEP 4: REFLECT**
   - Daily: Review calendar and next actions
   - Weekly: Complete Weekly Review (detailed below)
   - Monthly: Review Someday/Maybe, projects

   **STEP 5: ENGAGE**
   - Four criteria for choosing what to do now:
     1. Context: Can I do this here/now? (@computer vs @errands)
     2. Time available: 5 mins or 2 hours?
     3. Energy available: High or low?
     4. Priority: What's most important given above?
   - Trust your system and your intuition

3. **The Weekly Review** (Critical Habit)
   - When: Friday afternoon or Sunday evening (15-25 mins)
   - **GET CLEAR**:
     - Collect loose papers, materials
     - Process inbox to zero
     - Empty brain dump (add new items)
   - **GET CURRENT**:
     - Review Next Actions (is anything done? anything new?)
     - Review Projects (progress? next actions defined?)
     - Review Calendar (past 2 weeks, next 2 weeks)
     - Review Waiting For (follow up on delegated items?)
     - Review Someday/Maybe (anything ready to activate?)
   - **GET CREATIVE**:
     - Reflect on goals and values
     - Generate new ideas
     - Feel confident about what's NOT getting done
   - Outcome: Complete confidence you're working on right things

4. **Inbox Zero**
   - Process inbox, don't check it
   - Touch each item once
   - Empty to zero regularly (daily)
   - If it takes <2 mins, do it
   - If not, move to appropriate list

5. **Projects vs. Next Actions**
   - Project = Any outcome requiring >1 step
   - "Plan vacation" is a project
   - "Google hotels in Hawaii" is a next action
   - Every project must have ≥1 next action defined
   - Review projects weekly to ensure next actions exist

6. **Contexts**
   - Filter next actions by where/what you can do
   - Examples:
     - @computer
     - @phone
     - @errands
     - @office
     - @home
     - @waiting-for
     - @low-energy (for when you're tired)
     - @high-energy (for deep work)

7. **The 2-Minute Rule**
   - If it takes <2 mins, do it immediately
   - Faster than organizing it
   - Prevents small tasks from piling up
   - Applies during both Capture and Engage phases

8. **Someday/Maybe List**
   - Parking lot for ideas, not commitments
   - "Learn Spanish", "Write a book", "Visit Japan"
   - Review quarterly
   - Removes mental burden of forgotten dreams

9. **GTD Tools**
   - Paper: Notebook, folders, filing system
   - Digital: Todoist, Things, OmniFocus, Notion
   - Hybrid: Paper for capture, digital for organization
   - Choose what works for you (consistency matters more than tool)

10. **Common GTD Mistakes**
    - Skipping weekly review (system breaks down)
    - Next actions too vague ("Plan marketing" → "Draft email to designer about homepage")
    - Calendar clutter (only time-specific items)
    - Too many lists (keep it simple)
    - Perfectionism (done > perfect)

11. **GTD for Teams**
    - Shared Waiting For list
    - Shared projects list
    - Sync in standups
    - Respect each other's contexts

12. **Integration with Other Frameworks**
    - GTD + Eisenhower: Use matrix during weekly review
    - GTD + Time Blocking: Schedule next actions on calendar
    - GTD + OKRs: Projects align with key results

**Timeline**: 4-6 weeks to fully implement GTD system

### 12-pareto-principle-8020-rule.md

**Purpose**: Apply the 80/20 rule to identify and focus on high-leverage activities.

**Content:**

1. **The Pareto Principle**
   - Discovered by Vilfredo Pareto (1906): 80% of land in Italy owned by 20% of population
   - Generalized: 80% of results come from 20% of efforts
   - Not exactly 80/20 (could be 90/10 or 70/30), but the imbalance is universal

2. **Examples of 80/20 in Life and Work**
   - 80% of revenue from 20% of customers
   - 80% of product usage from 20% of features
   - 80% of complaints from 20% of customers
   - 80% of sales from 20% of salespeople
   - 80% of results from 20% of your time
   - 80% of happiness from 20% of activities
   - 80% of learning from 20% of reading

3. **Identifying Your Vital 20%**
   - **For tasks**: Which tasks create most impact? (Revenue, user value, personal fulfillment)
   - **For features**: Which features drive most usage/satisfaction?
   - **For customers**: Which customers generate most revenue/joy?
   - **For activities**: Which activities bring most energy/happiness?
   - **For relationships**: Which relationships matter most?
   - Method: Track and analyze for 1-2 weeks

4. **The 80/20 Analysis Process**
   - **Step 1**: List all items (tasks, features, customers, etc.)
   - **Step 2**: Measure impact (revenue, time saved, satisfaction, etc.)
   - **Step 3**: Rank by impact
   - **Step 4**: Calculate cumulative % of impact
   - **Step 5**: Identify top 20% that create 80% of impact
   - **Step 6**: Double down on top 20%, eliminate/minimize bottom 80%

5. **Applying 80/20 to Daily Tasks**
   - Morning exercise: List all tasks
   - Circle top 20% (highest impact)
   - Do those first
   - Batch/delegate/eliminate the rest
   - Example: 10 tasks → focus on top 2

6. **Applying 80/20 to Goals**
   - Which goals will create most life satisfaction?
   - Warren Buffett's 5/25 rule is 20/100 rule (top 5 of 25)
   - Focus on vital few, ignore trivial many

7. **Applying 80/20 to Product Features**
   - User analytics: Which features get 80% of usage?
   - Invest in those, deprioritize rest
   - Don't build more features—improve core features
   - Example: Instagram focused on photo filters (vital 20%) vs. all social features

8. **The Danger of the Trivial Many (80%)**
   - Easy to stay busy with low-impact work
   - Feels productive but isn't
   - Perfectionism on unimportant tasks
   - "Good" is the enemy of "great" (Jim Collins)

9. **Compounding 80/20**
   - Apply recursively: 20% of your 20% = 4% creates 64% of results
   - Extreme focus on extreme winners
   - Example: Top 4% of features may drive 64% of value

10. **When 80/20 Doesn't Apply**
    - Baseline requirements (must do 100%, e.g., compliance)
    - Relationships (not all transactional)
    - Ethical issues (can't ignore 80% of customer complaints)
    - Use judgment

11. **80/20 for Time Management**
    - 80% of your results come from 20% of your time
    - Which hours are most productive? (Biological prime time)
    - Which meetings create most value?
    - Eliminate bottom 80% of time wasters

12. **Case Studies**
    - Company that cut 70% of features, revenue increased
    - Person who quit 80% of commitments, happiness doubled
    - Sales team that focused on top 20% of customers (results)

**Timeline**: Apply immediately to current work

**Templates Referenced**: `pareto-analysis-template`, `8020-task-audit`

### 13-weekly-review-process.md

**Purpose**: Establish the critical habit of weekly reflection and planning.

**Content:**

1. **Why Weekly Review Matters**
   - Prevents priorities from drifting
   - Catches dropped balls before they hit the ground
   - Aligns tactics with strategy
   - Reduces stress ("I know what's on my plate")
   - Continuous improvement through reflection

2. **When to Schedule Your Weekly Review**
   - Friday afternoon (fresh in mind, plan for next week)
   - Sunday evening (clear head for Monday)
   - Whatever time you'll actually do it consistently
   - Non-negotiable calendar hold (30 mins)

3. **The Complete Weekly Review Process** (GTD-based)

   **PART 1: GET CLEAR (10 mins)**
   - Collect loose papers, receipts, notes
   - Process inboxes to zero (email, Slack, physical)
   - Empty brain (write down anything on your mind)

   **PART 2: GET CURRENT (10 mins)**
   - Review calendar:
     - Past 2 weeks (anything fall through cracks?)
     - Next 2 weeks (prepare for upcoming commitments)
   - Review next actions lists (anything done? anything to add?)
   - Review projects list (progress? stalled? next actions defined?)
   - Review waiting-for list (follow up on delegated items)
   - Review someday/maybe (anything ready to activate?)

   **PART 3: GET CREATIVE (5 mins)**
   - Reflect on bigger picture:
     - How did this week go?
     - What went well?
     - What didn't go well?
     - What do I want to focus on next week?
   - Brainstorm new ideas
   - Align with quarterly goals

   **PART 4: PLAN NEXT WEEK (5 mins)**
   - Identify 3-5 wins for next week (Q2 activities)
   - Time block deep work
   - Anticipate challenges
   - Feel confident

4. **Weekly Review Checklist**
   - [ ] Inboxes to zero (email, Slack, physical)
   - [ ] Brain dump complete
   - [ ] Calendar reviewed (past and upcoming)
   - [ ] Next actions updated
   - [ ] Projects reviewed (all have next actions)
   - [ ] Waiting-for followed up
   - [ ] Reflection questions answered
   - [ ] Next week planned (top 3-5 priorities)
   - [ ] Deep work time blocked

5. **Reflection Questions**
   - What am I grateful for this week?
   - What did I accomplish that I'm proud of?
   - What didn't get done that I thought would?
   - Where did I spend time that wasn't valuable?
   - Where did I procrastinate or avoid?
   - What's one thing I want to improve next week?
   - Am I working on the right things?
   - Am I aligned with my values and goals?

6. **Weekly Metrics to Track** (Optional)
   - Hours of deep work completed
   - % of top 3 priorities achieved
   - Number of times said "no"
   - Energy levels (1-10 average)
   - Stress levels (1-10 average)
   - Work/life balance satisfaction

7. **Building the Weekly Review Habit**
   - Week 1-2: 45 mins (learning process)
   - Week 3-4: 30 mins (getting efficient)
   - Week 5+: 15-25 mins (habit established)
   - Miss one? Do it immediately (don't let it slide)
   - Link to existing habit (Friday coffee ritual)

8. **Monthly Review** (Quarterly-lite)
   - Every 4th weekly review, go deeper (60 mins):
     - Review monthly goals
     - Review progress toward quarterly rocks
     - Update someday/maybe list
     - Prune completed projects
     - Audit time spent (Pareto analysis)

9. **Common Mistakes**
   - Skipping it ("too busy") → Causes more busy-ness
   - Rushing through (misses dropped balls)
   - Only reviewing, not planning ahead
   - No reflection (miss learning opportunities)
   - Inconsistent timing (habit never forms)

10. **Weekly Review for Teams**
    - Friday afternoon team check-in (30 mins)
    - Wins from this week
    - Challenges
    - Priorities for next week
    - Blockers or needs

**Timeline**: 15-25 minutes weekly (ongoing habit)

### 14-quarterly-annual-planning.md

**Purpose**: Set strategic direction through quarterly and annual planning cycles.

**Content:**

1. **Why Quarterly Planning?**
   - Annual plans are too long (stale by Q3)
   - Monthly plans are too short (tactical, not strategic)
   - 13 weeks (quarter) is perfect:
     - Long enough to accomplish something significant
     - Short enough to maintain urgency
     - Adapts to changing conditions 4x per year

2. **The 13-Week Year**
   - Think in 12-week or 13-week increments
   - More urgency than 52-week year
   - Research: People accomplish more in 12 weeks than many do in 12 months
   - Forces prioritization (can't fit everything)

3. **Quarterly Planning Process** (Half-Day)

   **STEP 1: REFLECT ON LAST QUARTER (30 mins)**
   - Review last quarter's rocks/priorities
   - What got accomplished?
   - What didn't? Why?
   - What surprised you?
   - What did you learn?
   - Celebrate wins

   **STEP 2: REVIEW ANNUAL GOALS (15 mins)**
   - Where are you on annual objectives?
   - Still relevant? Need adjustment?
   - Which goals need focus this quarter?

   **STEP 3: BRAINSTORM QUARTERLY PRIORITIES (30 mins)**
   - What's most important next 13 weeks?
   - Both professional and personal
   - Brain dump all possibilities (20-30 items)

   **STEP 4: SELECT QUARTERLY ROCKS (30 mins)**
   - "Rocks" = top 3-5 priorities for quarter (from Traction/EOS framework)
   - Use 5/25 rule thinking: Can only focus on vital few
   - Criteria:
     - Aligns with annual goals
     - High impact
     - Achievable in 13 weeks
     - You control it (not dependent on others)
   - Write each rock as SMART goal

   **STEP 5: DEFINE SUCCESS METRICS (15 mins)**
   - How will you know you achieved each rock?
   - Specific, measurable outcomes

   **STEP 6: BREAK DOWN INTO MILESTONES (30 mins)**
   - Each rock: What needs to happen by end of Month 1, 2, 3?
   - Identify first next actions

   **STEP 7: ANTICIPATE OBSTACLES (15 mins)**
   - What could prevent success?
   - How will you mitigate?

   **STEP 8: COMMIT (5 mins)**
   - Write rocks where you'll see them daily
   - Share with accountability partner
   - Schedule check-ins (weekly)

4. **Annual Planning Process** (Full Day)

   **STEP 1: YEAR IN REVIEW (60 mins)**
   - Reflect on past year across life domains:
     - Career: Wins, growth, challenges
     - Finances: Progress, setbacks
     - Health: Physical, mental
     - Relationships: Family, friends, romantic
     - Personal growth: Learning, hobbies
     - Contribution: Impact on others
   - What am I grateful for?
   - What did I accomplish?
   - What do I regret?
   - What did I learn?

   **STEP 2: VALUES CHECK-IN (30 mins)**
   - Review core values (from 01-values-clarification-foundation.md)
   - Still accurate?
   - How aligned was last year? (Values Bulls-Eye)

   **STEP 3: VISION CASTING (45 mins)**
   - Where do you want to be in 1 year? 3 years? 10 years?
   - Visualization exercise
   - Write your "perfect day" 1 year from now
   - What does success look like?

   **STEP 4: SET ANNUAL THEMES/PRIORITIES (60 mins)**
   - Choose 1-3 themes for the year ("Year of Health", "Year of Focus", "Year of Building")
   - Set 3-5 annual goals (SMART format)
   - Personal and professional balance
   - Use 5/25 rule

   **STEP 5: BREAK INTO QUARTERLY ROCKS (45 mins)**
   - Rough plan for each quarter:
     - Q1: Which rocks?
     - Q2: Which rocks?
     - Q3: Which rocks?
     - Q4: Which rocks?
   - Sequence thoughtfully (what needs to happen first?)

   **STEP 6: IDENTIFY WHAT TO STOP (30 mins)**
   - To make room for new priorities
   - What commitments to drop?
   - What habits to eliminate?
   - What relationships to de-prioritize?
   - Saying no list

   **STEP 7: SYSTEMS & HABITS (30 mins)**
   - What systems will support your goals?
   - What habits to build?
   - What routines to establish?

   **STEP 8: COMMITMENT (15 mins)**
   - Write it down
   - Share with partner/friend
   - Schedule quarterly reviews

5. **Quarterly Check-Ins** (During Weekly Reviews)
   - Week 1 of quarter: Review rocks, high energy
   - Week 4-5: Mid-quarter check (on track?)
   - Week 9-10: Push to finish line
   - Week 13: Reflect and plan next quarter

6. **Adjusting Mid-Quarter**
   - Quarterly doesn't mean rigid
   - If rock becomes irrelevant, adjust
   - Better to change direction than pursue wrong goal
   - But don't abandon at first sign of difficulty

7. **Balancing Personal and Professional**
   - Many people set only work goals
   - Include personal rocks:
     - Health: "Run 3x per week"
     - Relationships: "Weekly date night"
     - Learning: "Complete online course"
     - Fun: "Plan 2 weekend trips"

8. **The Power of Seasonal Thinking**
   - Some quarters emphasize work (product launch quarter)
   - Some quarters emphasize life (summer family time)
   - Intentional imbalance across year creates overall balance

9. **Tools for Planning**
   - Notion, Asana, or spreadsheet
   - Physical journal/planner
   - Vision board (visual rocks)
   - Accountability group

10. **Common Mistakes**
    - Too many rocks (more than 5)
    - Rocks too vague (not SMART)
    - Forgetting personal life
    - Setting and forgetting (no weekly check-ins)
    - Continuing pursuit of irrelevant goals
    - No reflection on previous period

**Timeline**: Half-day quarterly, full day annually

**Templates Referenced**: `quarterly-planning-template`, `annual-planning-template`, `year-in-review-worksheet`, `quarterly-rocks-template`
