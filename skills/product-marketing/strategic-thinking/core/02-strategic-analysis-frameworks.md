## Framework Overview

Strategic analysis frameworks help you:
- **Understand your current position**: Strengths, weaknesses, competitive dynamics
- **Identify opportunities and threats**: Market trends, emerging competitors, disruptions
- **Make informed decisions**: Based on structured analysis, not just intuition
- **Communicate insights**: Frameworks create shared language for strategic discussions

**Key principle**: Frameworks are thinking tools, not rigid formulas. Adapt them to your context and combine multiple frameworks for comprehensive analysis.

## SWOT Analysis

**When to use**: Quick assessment of current position, strategy kickoff, annual planning

**What it is**: Structured analysis of Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats

### How to Conduct SWOT Analysis

**Step 1: Define scope**
- What are you analyzing? (Product, business, feature, career, initiative)
- What timeframe? (Current state, next quarter, next year)
- Who should participate? (Individual, team, cross-functional)

**Step 2: Gather internal factors** (Strengths and Weaknesses)

**Strengths**: Internal attributes that give you advantage
- What do you do better than competitors?
- What unique resources or capabilities do you have?
- What do customers cite as reasons they choose you?
- What assets or intellectual property do you own?

**Example questions**:
- What's our strongest competitive differentiator?
- What capabilities could we leverage more?
- What relationships or partnerships are valuable?

**Weaknesses**: Internal limitations that put you at disadvantage
- What do competitors do better than you?
- What resources or capabilities are you missing?
- What do customers complain about?
- What causes you to lose deals?

**Example questions**:
- Where do we consistently underperform?
- What gaps in our product/team/capabilities exist?
- What would we change if we could start over?

**Step 3: Identify external factors** (Opportunities and Threats)

**Opportunities**: External conditions you could exploit
- What market trends favor your approach?
- What unmet customer needs exist?
- What gaps in competitive landscape could you fill?
- What technologies or partnerships could accelerate growth?

**Example questions**:
- What's changing in our favor in the market?
- What emerging customer segments could we serve?
- What adjacent markets could we enter?

**Threats**: External conditions that could harm you
- What competitive moves threaten your position?
- What market trends work against you?
- What regulatory or economic changes could hurt you?
- What technologies could disrupt your business?

**Example questions**:
- What keeps you up at night about competition?
- What could make our product/approach obsolete?
- What external forces are outside our control?

**Step 4: Analyze and prioritize**

Create SWOT matrix:

```
INTERNAL          | POSITIVE              | NEGATIVE
FACTORS           | (Helpful)             | (Harmful)
------------------|-----------------------|----------------------
                  | STRENGTHS             | WEAKNESSES
                  | - [List 3-5]          | - [List 3-5]
                  | - Unique capability   | - Resource gaps
                  | - Brand strength      | - Technical debt
                  | - Customer loyalty    | - Limited reach
------------------|-----------------------|----------------------
EXTERNAL          | OPPORTUNITIES         | THREATS
FACTORS           | - [List 3-5]          | - [List 3-5]
                  | - Market growth       | - New competitors
                  | - Emerging segments   | - Price pressure
                  | - Technology trends   | - Regulation
```

**Step 5: Develop strategic actions**

**SO Strategies** (Strengths + Opportunities):
- How can you use strengths to capitalize on opportunities?
- Example: "Use our AI expertise to enter emerging ML tools market"

**WO Strategies** (Weaknesses + Opportunities):
- How can you overcome weaknesses to pursue opportunities?
- Example: "Partner with established brand to enter enterprise market despite our startup status"

**ST Strategies** (Strengths + Threats):
- How can you use strengths to defend against threats?
- Example: "Leverage customer loyalty to prevent churn to new competitor"

**WT Strategies** (Weaknesses + Threats):
- How can you minimize weaknesses and avoid threats?
- Example: "Outsource areas where we're weak to focus on core differentiation"

### SWOT Best Practices

‚úÖ **DO**:
- Be specific and honest (avoid vague statements)
- Gather diverse perspectives (cross-functional input)
- Focus on the most important items (3-7 per quadrant, not 20)
- Update regularly (quarterly for dynamic markets)
- Connect SWOT to specific actions

‚ùå **DON'T**:
- Confuse weaknesses with threats (internal vs. external)
- List only obvious items (dig deeper for insights)
- Create SWOT and file it away (use it to drive decisions)
- Overestimate strengths or underestimate threats (be realistic)

## Porter's Five Forces

**When to use**: Industry analysis, competitive strategy development, market entry decisions

**What it is**: Framework for understanding competitive intensity and profitability of an industry

### The Five Forces

**1. Competitive Rivalry** (existing competitors)
- How intense is competition among current players?
- How many competitors? How similar are they?
- What's the rate of industry growth?
- What are exit barriers keeping players in the market?

**Analyze**:
- Number of competitors and market concentration
- Product/service differentiation
- Price competition vs. value competition
- Marketing spend and competitive moves

**Strategic implications**:
- High rivalry ‚Üí Focus on differentiation, find niches, build switching costs
- Low rivalry ‚Üí Opportunity for profitability, but attracts new entrants

**2. Threat of New Entrants**
- How easy is it for new competitors to enter the market?
- What barriers to entry exist?
- How would incumbents react to new entrants?

**Analyze**:
- Capital requirements (high = barrier)
- Economies of scale (favor incumbents)
- Brand loyalty and switching costs
- Access to distribution channels
- Regulatory requirements
- Proprietary technology or IP

**Strategic implications**:
- Low barriers ‚Üí Build moats (switching costs, network effects, brand)
- High barriers ‚Üí Protect barriers, leverage scale advantages

**3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
- How much power do suppliers have over you?
- Can they raise prices or reduce quality?

**Analyze**:
- Number of suppliers (few = high power)
- Uniqueness of supplier products/services
- Switching costs to alternative suppliers
- Supplier ability to forward integrate (become competitors)

**Strategic implications**:
- High supplier power ‚Üí Diversify suppliers, vertical integration, partnerships
- Low supplier power ‚Üí Negotiate favorable terms, optimize supply chain

**4. Bargaining Power of Buyers** (customers)
- How much power do customers have?
- Can they demand lower prices or better terms?

**Analyze**:
- Customer concentration (few large customers = high power)
- Price sensitivity of customers
- Switching costs for customers
- Product differentiation
- Customer profitability (are margins high enough to sustain pressure?)

**Strategic implications**:
- High buyer power ‚Üí Differentiate, increase switching costs, serve multiple segments
- Low buyer power ‚Üí Capture value through pricing, invest in product

**5. Threat of Substitutes**
- What alternatives could customers use instead of your product?
- How easily can customers switch to substitutes?

**Analyze**:
- Availability of substitutes
- Price-performance of substitutes
- Switching costs to substitutes
- Customer propensity to substitute

**Strategic implications**:
- High substitute threat ‚Üí Differentiate on unique value, increase switching costs
- Low substitute threat ‚Üí Opportunity for premium pricing, focus on quality

### How to Apply Porter's Five Forces

**Step 1: Assess each force** (rate as Low, Medium, High)

**Step 2: Identify most important forces**
- Which 2-3 forces most impact industry profitability?
- Which forces are changing and how?

**Step 3: Develop strategic responses**

Example analysis for B2B SaaS:

```
Force                        | Rating | Strategic Response
-----------------------------|--------|----------------------------------
Competitive Rivalry          | High   | Differentiate through AI features
Threat of New Entrants       | Medium | Build network effects, high NRR
Supplier Power (cloud, etc.) | Low    | Multi-cloud strategy
Buyer Power                  | High   | Create switching costs, integrations
Threat of Substitutes        | Medium | Expand platform, solve more jobs
```

**Step 4: Combine with SWOT**
- Porter's Five Forces = external environment analysis
- SWOT = internal + external analysis
- Use together for comprehensive view

## PESTEL Analysis

**When to use**: Long-term strategic planning, market entry decisions, scenario planning

**What it is**: Framework for analyzing macro-environmental factors

### The Six Factors

**Political**
- Government stability and policies
- Trade regulations and tariffs
- Tax policies
- Labor laws
- Political risks

**Economic**
- Economic growth rates
- Inflation and interest rates
- Exchange rates and currency stability
- Unemployment levels
- Disposable income trends

**Social**
- Demographics and population trends
- Cultural attitudes and values
- Lifestyle changes
- Education levels
- Social mobility

**Technological**
- Technology adoption rates
- R&D investment
- Automation and AI trends
- Infrastructure quality
- Emerging technologies

**Environmental**
- Climate change impacts
- Sustainability requirements
- Resource scarcity
- Waste management
- Carbon regulations

**Legal**
- Data privacy laws (GDPR, CCPA)
- Industry regulations
- Intellectual property protection
- Consumer protection laws
- Employment laws

### How to Conduct PESTEL Analysis

**Step 1: Identify relevant factors**
- List 3-5 items per category that affect your business
- Focus on factors you can't control but must respond to

**Step 2: Assess impact and likelihood**

```
Factor                    | Impact | Likelihood | Timeframe | Response
--------------------------|--------|------------|-----------|----------
AI regulation increases   | High   | High       | 2-3 years | Build compliance
Remote work continues     | Medium | High       | Ongoing   | Expand talent pool
Economic recession        | High   | Medium     | 1-2 years | Diversify customers
```

**Step 3: Identify strategic implications**
- Which factors create opportunities?
- Which pose threats?
- What scenarios should you plan for?

**Step 4: Integrate into strategy**
- Update strategy based on macro trends
- Build flexibility for uncertain factors
- Monitor changes continuously

### PESTEL Best Practices

‚úÖ Focus on factors specific to your industry and geography
‚úÖ Update annually or when major changes occur
‚úÖ Combine with Porter's Five Forces for complete external analysis
‚úÖ Use for scenario planning (what if X factor changes significantly?)

‚ùå Don't list every possible factor (focus on high-impact items)
‚ùå Don't ignore uncomfortable factors (face threats directly)
‚ùå Don't analyze without acting (connect to strategic decisions)

## Customer & Market Analysis

### Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework

**When to use**: Product strategy, positioning, feature prioritization

**Core principle**: Customers don't buy products, they "hire" them to do jobs

**How to identify jobs**:

1. **Interview customers**: "What were you trying to accomplish when you bought our product?"
2. **Identify the job**: Functional, emotional, and social jobs
3. **Map the job**: Circumstances, desired outcomes, obstacles

**Jobs-to-Be-Done Statement Template**:

"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

**Example**: "When I'm preparing for a customer meeting, I want to quickly find relevant case studies, so I can build credibility and win the deal."

**Strategic application**:
- Build features that help customers complete jobs better
- Position against competitors based on job performance
- Identify new markets (other people with same jobs)

### Market Segmentation

**How to segment markets**:

**1. Demographic**: Age, company size, industry, role, location
**2. Psychographic**: Values, attitudes, lifestyle, goals
**3. Behavioral**: Usage patterns, engagement, purchase behavior
**4. Needs-based**: Different jobs or problems to solve

**Strategic segmentation template**:

```
Segment Name: [Descriptive name]

Size: [Market size, # of potential customers]
Characteristics:
- [Defining attribute 1]
- [Defining attribute 2]
- [Defining attribute 3]

Jobs/Needs:
- [Primary job to be done]
- [Secondary jobs]

Value to us:
- Willingness to pay: [High/Medium/Low]
- Acquisition cost: [High/Medium/Low]
- Lifetime value: [High/Medium/Low]
- Strategic fit: [High/Medium/Low]

How to reach them:
- Channels: [Where they are]
- Messages: [What resonates]
- Objections: [What they worry about]
```

## Systems Thinking

**When to use**: Complex problems, unintended consequences, long-term planning

**Core concepts**:

**1. Interconnections**: Everything affects everything else
- Map relationships between parts of the system
- Identify feedback loops

**2. Feedback Loops**:
- **Reinforcing loops**: Growth or decline accelerates (virtuous or vicious cycles)
- **Balancing loops**: Self-correcting mechanisms that maintain stability

**3. Delays**: Time gaps between cause and effect
- Actions taken today may not show results for months/years
- Plan for delays in your strategy

### How to Apply Systems Thinking

**Step 1: Map the system**

```
[Customer satisfaction] ‚Üí [Retention] ‚Üí [Revenue] ‚Üí [Investment in product]
                             ‚Üë                              |
                             |______________________________|
                          (Reinforcing feedback loop)
```

**Step 2: Identify leverage points**
- Where can small changes create big impacts?
- What feedback loops can you strengthen or weaken?

**Step 3: Anticipate unintended consequences**
- If we do X, what else might happen?
- What second-order and third-order effects should we consider?

**Example**: Aggressive growth strategy
- Direct effect: More customers
- Second-order effect: Support team overwhelmed
- Third-order effect: Customer satisfaction drops, churn increases
- System effect: Growth undermines itself

**Strategic response**: Invest in support scaling BEFORE aggressive growth

## First Principles Thinking

**When to use**: Innovation, challenging industry norms, cost reduction, breakthrough strategies

**Process**:

**Step 1: Identify and define your current assumptions**
- What do we believe must be true?
- What constraints do we accept?

**Step 2: Break down the problem into fundamental truths**
- What is absolutely true?
- What is just "conventional wisdom"?

**Step 3: Create new solutions from scratch**
- If we didn't have these constraints, what would we do?
- What's possible if we rebuild from fundamentals?

**Example: Traditional vs. First Principles**

**Traditional thinking**: "We need sales reps to sell enterprise software"
**First principles**:
- Fundamental truth: Enterprise buyers need to understand value and trust the solution
- Question: Do sales reps uniquely provide this, or are there other ways?
- Insight: Product-led growth with strong self-serve + great product can build trust
- Innovation: PLG motion for enterprise (Slack, Figma model)

| Situation | Best Framework(s) |
|-----------|------------------|

- JTBD ensures customer-centricity
- Systems thinking reveals interconnections
